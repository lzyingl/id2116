. three .

intro to p5.js

brainstorming for our everyday object

We chose the pencil case as our everyday object because we saw potential in the motion of sliding/pulling the zip. Our initial ideas were to use the motion to play a video, with simple open=start; close=stop commands. We had our minds fixed on using a motion sensor or accelerometer to record the motion, but we then realised that using a light sensor to detect the open/close state of the pencil case would be more straightforward and possibly more reliable to work with.

. four .

putting it together: micro:bit & p5.js

initial ideation

When brainstorming for the visual feedback we wanted to work on, our aim was to make it interesting and relevant to our motion. One of our ideas involved the direct mapping of the sliding motion onto the visual feedback of a sliding colour palette. The end result would be similar to a colouring book, where each part is highlighted for the user to select a specific colour using the colour slider, working towards a fully personalized coloured image. We also considered additional sensors, such as pressure points, to communicate a final selection after the slider hovers over an option.

final ideation: a bubble blowing pencil case

After further brainstorming, we realised that turning the physical sliding motion into a digital sliding motion is not ideally interesting. A more fascinating project would involve a visual feedback that is pleasantly unexpected but would make sense with the physical interaction. Our final idea was to relate the unzipping of a pencil case to a release of bubbles digitally reflected on the screen! We chose bubbles because we wanted to convey a feeling of lighthearted joy that would appeal to almost any age group, while hinting at a childlike sense of fun. In trying to make our project even more interactive, we decided to split the motion into at least two parts. This would create a more realistic representation of how the gradual increase in amount of bubbles released is dependent on how far the pencil case is unzipped.

experiment with micro:bit & external light sensor

We experimented many times to precisely record the amount of studio light that is registered when the pencil case is half open and fully open to determine the range input to command the two videos to play.
